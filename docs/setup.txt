Start by asking these questions:

 Stop Over-Engineering: The Grocery Store Guide to Architecture üçé
Choosing an architecture is a platform concern, not a feature. You can sell an apple using any of these three, but the "cost of the building" is what changes.

1. The Monolith (The Solo Shop)
The Scene: You do it all. You're the cashier, the cleaner, and the accountant.

The Technical Reality: One project, one database. Simple.

The Catch: If you get sick, the shop closes. If 50 people walk in, you panic.

Verdict: Great for starting. Don't build a stadium for a 1-man show.

2. The Modular Monolith (The Department Store)
The Scene: One big building, but you‚Äôve got "zones." A Bakery desk, a Meat counter, and a separate Checkout.

The Technical Reality: Different projects in one solution. They can talk to each other easily because they are under one roof.

The Catch: You still share one electric grid and one plumbing system (The Database).

Verdict: The Sweet Spot. It keeps your code clean without the "Microservice Tax."

3. Microservices (The Separate Factory)
The Scene: Your Bakery is so busy it needs its own industrial oven and a dedicated power line. So, you move the Bakery to a separate building across town.

The Technical Reality: The Bakery is now its own app with its own database. It sends bread to the main store via a truck (The Network).

The Catch: If the truck gets stuck in traffic (Network Lag), the store has no bread. It‚Äôs expensive and complex to manage.

Verdict: Only do this for the parts of your app that are "exploding" and need their own "power grid."

The Moral of the Story
You don‚Äôt need a Ferrari to go to the grocery store two blocks away.

Microservices are an optimization, not a starting point.

Most apps fail because they were so "future-proof" they required 10 DevOps engineers just to open the front door.

Rule of thumb: Start with a Modular Monolith. It keeps your "departments" organized from day one. If your Bakery ever gets too big for the building, you can ship it off to its own factory later.
